# European Orthography: Conversation Notes and Book Plan

Captured from a conversation on 2026-04-19 while working on AztecCal. The conversation drifted from a stray comment about the English spelling of "separation" into a multi-hour tour of European orthographic development, reform, and politics. This document preserves the substantive material, organized for future use as research toward a potential retirement book.

## Core Synthesis: The Frontier / Interior / Purge Typology

The most useful framework that emerged. Orthographic and lexical reform operates under three distinct regimes, each with its own conditions for success:

- **Frontier regime.** Protection works here. Institutions can shape which new loans enter the language. Example: Quebec's OQLF resisting new English loans into Quebec French.
- **Interior regime.** Reform and purge both fail here. Words embedded deeply enough are effectively immovable. Examples: Romanian *da* surviving the 19th-century Latinist purge; Quebec *fun* surviving Bill 101.
- **Purge regime.** Active removal of existing loans works only when the target is both **recent** and **politically charged**. Examples: post-1989 Romanian de-russification (*soviet*, *kolhoz*, *politburo* dropped quickly); Atatürk's 1928 Turkish script and vocabulary reforms.

**Implication for policy**: a protectionist agency should triage explicitly by regime rather than treating all borrowings as a single class. Agencies that do this implicitly (OQLF) survive and are effective. Agencies that attempt to fight the interior (the extremist fringe of any purification movement) lose credibility and fail. Agencies that intervene only at the frontier but don't describe their logic get mischaracterized.

## Case Studies by Pattern

### Nationalist Archaization

- **Greek Katharevousa.** Adamantios Korais (1748–1833), working in Enlightenment Paris, proposed Katharevousa (literally "purifying") as a reach backward to Classical and Koine Greek, explicitly political — restoring linguistic independence Ottoman rule had eroded. Reinforced by Western European philhellenism (Byron, Shelley) framing Greeks as "heirs of Homer" and by the Megali Idea needing a continuity narrative from antiquity through Byzantium to the modern state. Demotic wins only in 1976, once the Ottoman trauma had cooled enough for archaizing performance to stop being necessary.
- **Faroese Hammershaimb orthography (1846).** The clearest documented blank-slate case. V.U. Hammershaimb explicitly chose Old Norse etymology over Jens Christian Svabo's earlier phonetic-orthography proposal — specifically so that written Faroese would look Icelandic and assert Norse heritage against Danish cultural subordination. Modern result: spelling opacity comparable to Danish, but by conscious choice rather than drift.
- **Hebrew revival.** Ideology (Ben-Yehuda's Sephardic pronunciation target) lost to phonology (adult Ashkenazi pioneers couldn't retrain their uvular R). The first native-Israeli-Hebrew generation acquired what their parents physically produced. Uvular R in Modern Hebrew is the single major phonological feature where Ashkenazi won over the explicit Sephardic target.
- **Turkish language reform (1928).** Atatürk's Arabic-to-Latin script change plus aggressive purge of Arabic and Persian loanwords toward an imagined pre-Islamic Turkic origin. The most successful authoritarian-imposed reform in the modern era.
- **Finnish nationalism (19th century).** Folk-vernacular revival, Kalevala-era vocabulary, distinguishing from Swedish dominance.
- **Catalan Renaixença.** Medieval Catalan revived against Castilian Spanish.

### Latinist Purification

- **Romanian, 19th century.** The Transylvanian School (*Școala Ardeleană*) — Samuil Micu, Gheorghe Șincai, Petru Maior, Greek Catholic clerics trained in Rome and Vienna — argued the Daco-Roman continuity theory: Romanians as descendants of Roman colonists submerged under Slavic contact. Cyrillic replaced by Latin alphabet (1862). Slavic vocabulary replaced with Latin and French borrowings. But deep vocabulary survived: *da, prieten* (friend), *nevastă* (wife), *trebuie* (must), *iubi* (to love) — all Slavic, all too embedded to dislodge. Laurian's extreme Latinist dictionary (1871–76) so aggressive it was unreadable; Maiorescu's Junimea society rejected the extreme and argued for moderation. The purge succeeded in moderated form — Latinized surface, Slavic-colored intimate register — leaving modern Romanian at ~30% Slavic vocabulary, down from an estimated pre-reform 50%.
- **Italian's pre-modern Italianization of Latinate spellings.** *Philosophia* → *filosofia*, *theoria* → *teoria*, Latin *y* → *i*, "ph" and "th" digraphs dropped. This happened during the late Medieval and Renaissance periods, centuries before unification. Compare French, which kept *philosophie* and *théorie*. Same Romance ancestor, opposite orthographic choice, driven by Dante/Petrarch/Boccaccio's vernacular-based literary tradition.

### Failed or Partial Reform

- **Danish.** The 1948 reform removed noun-capitalization (shedding a German convention) and changed *aa* → *å*. But Danish pronunciation has drifted dramatically since the 17th century — consonant lenition (intervocalic /b, d, g/ → fricatives or silent), vowel reduction, /r/ as a pharyngeal vowel-colorer, unwritten stød — without orthographic follow-through. Reasons for the gap: literary continuity with Kierkegaard, Andersen, Holberg; cross-Scandinavian written legibility with Norwegian Bokmål and Swedish; elite comfort with the current system; no political crisis forcing reform.
- **Norwegian Bokmål (contrast).** Did reform aggressively because Norway needed to distinguish from Danish after 1814 independence. Knud Knudsen from the 1850s onward, continuing through 1917, 1938, and 1981 revisions. Denmark lacked the equivalent political motive. Norwegian Bokmål is what Danish spelling would look like today if Denmark had needed one.
- **French 1990 rectifications orthographiques.** Modest — circumflex accents removed from most *i* and *u*, some hyphenation adjusted, plural of compounds regularized. Submitted as recommendations, not rules. Widely ignored by publishers, schools, and users. Demonstrates that a centralized authority without enforcement power can't push even modest reform through.
- **Greek 1982 monotonic.** Replaced the ancient polytonic accent system with a single accent plus diaeresis. Fiercely contested at the time, now universal. But the /i/ spelling question (six distinct orthographic representations: η, ι, υ, ει, οι, υι, all pronounced /i/) was not touched. Accents were politically tractable; deep vowel-spelling reform was not.

### Successful Inheritance Rather Than Reform

- **Italian.** The 19th-century unification *ratified* a convergence that had been accumulating for 500 years through the Florentine literary tradition. Dante's *De vulgari eloquentia* (c. 1305) had already argued for vernacular. Manzoni's *I promessi sposi* (1827, revised 1840) cemented modern Florentine as the basis. Italian's relatively transparent orthography is a side effect of this accident — the literary prestige language was vernacular, so there was no Latin-etymologizing authority to preserve archaic spellings.
- **Spanish.** Continuous regularization by the Real Academia Española since 1815. Multiple small reforms gradually bringing spelling into closer alignment with pronunciation. Works because the central authority is genuinely active.
- **Swedish 1906.** Removed silent *h* (*hvad* → *vad*, *hvem* → *vem*), regularized vowel doubling. Moderate scope, accepted by publishers and schools, successful. Contrast with Danish, which did not go through an equivalent reform.

### Blank-Slate Design

- **Faroese (Hammershaimb 1846)** stands alone as a case where orthographic choice was made deliberately, against a named alternative (Svabo's phonetic proposal), with full knowledge of the trade-off. Most other "designed" orthographies (Turkish, Korean Hangul, various colonial alphabets) were imposed top-down; Faroese was chosen by the linguistic community from competing proposals.
- **Glagolitic (Cyril, c. 863 CE).** A second blank-slate case worth pairing with Faroese. Cyril designed Glagolitic for the Moravian mission to give Old Church Slavonic a written form. Not Greek-derived despite Cyril's Byzantine Greek background — the letterforms are distinctive and rounded, possibly drawing on Samaritan, Coptic, and Hebrew models. Unlike Faroese, Glagolitic did not survive in broad use: after Cyril's and Methodius's deaths, disciples in Bulgaria created a Greek-uncial-based replacement (what became Cyrillic) and named it after Cyril, even though the new alphabet was not his design. Glagolitic persisted only in Croatian Catholic liturgy into the 20th century. See also the "Fame Tracks Transmission" section below.

### Identity-Marker Orthographies: The Balkans as Concentrated Lab

In a small geographic area speaking variants of one language — **BCMS** (Bosnian, Croatian, Montenegrin, Serbian), all essentially Shtokavian — three scripts have operated simultaneously, each tracking religious and political identity rather than any structural linguistic feature:

- **Latin alphabet** — Croatian (Catholic, Habsburg and Venetian orbit); Bosnian and Montenegrin (more recent adoption).
- **Cyrillic alphabet** — Serbian (Orthodox, Byzantine and Ottoman orbit, refined by Vuk Karadžić's 19th-century phonetic reform: "write as you speak, read as written"); Montenegrin officially accepts both scripts.
- **Arabic alphabet (Arebica)** — historically Bosnian Muslim, before settling on Latin in modern times.

Same language, four slight standard variants, three active historical scripts, driven almost entirely by religious-political identity. Karadžić's Cyrillic reform is a nice secondary point: making the script more phonetic and internally simple *strengthened* its identity function, because it made Serbian visually and structurally distinctive from Croatian Latin even though the spoken language was the same.

The Croatian **Glagolitic tradition** is a unique case within this picture — a Catholic Slavic-liturgical script (neither Cyrillic-derived nor Latin-derived) that served as a bridge between Latin-Catholic identity and Slavic-speaking communities on the Dalmatian coast. The last Glagolitic missal was published in 1893; the script persisted in some Croatian Catholic churches into the 20th century. When Glagolitic waned, the Catholic framework made Latin the natural successor rather than Cyrillic — **religious affiliation overriding any structural similarity to Serbian**.

**Why this is the cleanest case for the "orthography as identity marker" claim**: the linguistic substrate (BCMS) is essentially constant across all the variation. Whenever orthographic difference elsewhere in Europe correlates with a structural linguistic difference, you can argue the spelling simply tracks the language. Here there's no such confound — one language, three scripts, every bit of variation driven by religion and political alignment. The Balkans are the cleanest demonstration in Europe of orthography functioning as pure identity marker. Every other case study has at least some linguistic cover for the choice; the Balkan case strips that cover away.

## Recurring Patterns and Principles

### Orthography as Multi-Purpose Cultural Artifact

Any orthographic system is simultaneously doing: phonetic representation, literary-continuity preservation, political-identity marking, morphological disambiguation, prestige signaling, cross-community legibility. Reducing the analysis to a single frame (usually "does the spelling match the sound?") misses what's actually happening. The anthropological reading applied to writing systems is the most productive analytical move.

### Demographics Determine Standard (the Hebrew Principle)

Who produces the first generation of native speakers sets the phonology, regardless of stated ideological preference. Ben-Yehuda himself — the ideologue of Sephardic pronunciation — almost certainly used uvular R because he was a native Lithuanian Yiddish speaker. The revival locked in what the founding demographic could physically produce, not what they prescribed. Counterfactual: had early Zionism's demographic center been New York rather than Odessa, Modern Hebrew might well use English approximant R.

### Ease of Articulation Drives Phonological Change

The Great R Shift across Europe (17th–19th century): alveolar trill → uvular in French, German, Portuguese, Dutch, Swedish. The trill is intrinsically unstable — hard to learn, hard to produce reliably even for natives. Spanish-speaking children show persistent *rotacismo*, typically acquiring the trill only at ages 4–6, sometimes later. Hebrew's Ashkenazi pioneers couldn't retrain this consonant as adults. The phoneme is genuinely harder than most, and languages drift away from it when the prestige conditions permit.

### Orthography Preserves Information Phonology Has Lost

- Greek /i/ spellings encode grammatical gender/number/person (masculine plural *-οι*, feminine singular *-η*, verb endings *-ει/-εις*).
- Danish silent consonants preserve morphological distinctions the pronunciation has erased.
- French silent verb endings (*-s, -nt*) carry person/number information — though **redundantly**, since French is strict non-pro-drop and the pronoun already carries it. The functional argument for conservative spelling is strongest where the phonology has lost information, weakest where the redundancy is complete (French).

### Etymology as Spelling Heuristic

The French-pronunciation trick for English spelling: *séparation* with its clear /a/ disambiguates *separation* vs. *seperation*. Works for roughly 60% of English vocabulary that comes from Latin or French (counting French intermediaries). Doesn't work for the Germanic half, which spells more phonetically anyway and doesn't benefit from etymological backtracking.

Related etymology-based tools:
- *Definite* (Latin *definitus*) — no 'a'.
- *Privilege* (Latin *privilegium*) — no 'd'.
- *Accommodate* (Latin *ad-* + *commodus*) — double *c*, double *m*, both from the prefix-plus-stem analysis.
- *Occurrence* — double *r* from the *currere* (running) stem.

### The -dge / -ge Pattern

Not purely etymological but emerged from Germanic palatalization patterns. Modern rule:
- Short vowel + /dʒ/ in stressed syllable → **-dge** (*bridge, ledge, judge, lodge*).
- Long vowel / diphthong / consonant + /dʒ/ → **-ge** (*cage, huge, large, hinge*).
- Latinate multi-syllable → **-ge** (*privilege, college, prestige*).

*Judge* is Romance-origin (Old French *juge*, Latin *judex*) but spelled with the Germanic -dge pattern because the vowel is short. The convention's origin was Germanic; its generalization is phonological. Useful disambiguation between two rules needed for English spelling: the etymology heuristic for Latinate multi-syllables, the vowel-length rule for monosyllables.

### English Preserves Medieval French Sounds

The /dʒ/ in *judge* and the /tʃ/ in *church* are sounds Old French had and modern French no longer has. English borrowed them during the Norman and post-Norman period; French then simplified /dʒ/ → /ʒ/ and /tʃ/ → /ʃ/ between the 13th and 16th centuries. Modern English is thus phonologically closer to medieval French than modern French is, at least for these specific affricates.

French is currently **re-importing /dʒ/** from two directions:
- **English loans**: *jazz, jogging, jean, job, DJ* — spelled with native English *j* but pronounced /dʒ/. Accepted spelling/sound mismatch.
- **Arabic loans**: *djinn, djihad, djebel, Djibouti, hadj* — spelled with explicit *dj* digraph to flag the non-native pronunciation.

French orthography has acquired dual conventions for /dʒ/, asymmetrically applied: *j* for English loans, *dj* for Arabic/Slavic loans. This is the context the *cràcher* proposal addresses.

### The Cràcher Proposal

From racecondition.software/blog/spelling/: proposes French *crasher* (to crash) → *cràcher*. Two moves:
1. Replace *sh* (not native French) with *ch* (native French representation of /ʃ/).
2. Mark the *a* with grave accent to disambiguate from *cracher* (to spit) — pure orthographic disambiguation, no phonetic work.

Demonstrates that:
- French orthography has the tools for systematic loan nativization (grave accent as disambiguator, *ch* digraph, *dj* for /dʒ/).
- The Académie française has the authority to act but has chosen not to.
- The historical precedent — *boulingrin* (bowling green), *redingote* (riding coat), *paletot* (Middle English *paltock*), *bifteck* (beefsteak), *poudingue* (pudding) — demonstrates that the convention used to be productive and simply stopped.
- English prestige is the force that stopped it. A 17th-century English loan carried no cultural charge; re-spelling it was natural. A modern loan carries signals of modernity and international chic; re-spelling it neuters the signal.

The proposal is a real reform suggestion, not a joke, and it foregrounds that the Académie's passivity is a choice rather than a necessity.

### The Anglosphere "Protective France" Myth

The popular narrative conflates three distinct things:
- **Académie française**: no enforcement power, forty elderly Immortals, glacial dictionary updates, mostly moral authority.
- **Toubon Law (1994)**: requires French in public/commercial contexts; doesn't give the Académie enforcement teeth.
- **Quebec's OQLF**: the actually-aggressive francophone institution, with legal teeth under Bill 101, compliance officers, fines, active terminology creation, and post-*fun* triage of frontier vs. interior cases.

Real francophone linguistic enforcement happens in Montreal, not Paris. The "protective France" narrative is usually describing Quebec while crediting France.

**The narrative as motte-and-bailey** (Nicholas Shackel, 2005). This myth fits Shackel's motte-and-bailey pattern cleanly, and naming the pattern gives the analysis a sharper spine:

- **Bailey (the attractive claim)**: France aggressively polices its language; the Académie française actively enforces French purity; French authorities restrict anglicisms through legal and cultural mechanisms.
- **Motte (the defensible retreat)**: France has a language academy that publishes guidance, and the Toubon Law requires French in some public contexts.

The motte is true. The bailey is a mischaracterization that slides from "these institutions exist" to "these institutions function as the narrative portrays." When challenged on specifics — *can* the Académie actually enforce anything? *does* the Toubon Law mandate orthographic reform? — defenders retreat to the motte, and the bailey quietly reasserts itself the next time the topic comes up. The chapter on this myth should make the slide explicit; naming the pattern also gives readers a tool for spotting similar moves in other linguistic-protectionism narratives (German Duden authority, Icelandic purism, the Turkish Language Association, the RAE, etc.). Motte-and-bailey is a useful enough analytical lens to deploy recurrently — it applies as well to the "Germanic is uniquely divergent" claim flagged in the McWhorter caveat, and to the "Aztec calendar" framing corrected in the Fame-Tracks-Transmission section. Worth introducing once, in a footnote with the Shackel citation, and then reusing as shorthand throughout.

### Fame Tracks Transmission, Not Innovation

A pattern about naming conventions, related to but distinct from the reform patterns. **The culture that becomes internationally famous for a cultural technology is often not the most sophisticated originator but the most effective consolidator at the moment of highest-visibility transmission to the eventual naming culture.** Fame attaches at the point of contact, not at the point of origination.

Case studies:

- **Arabic numerals → Indian numerals.** Decimal positional notation with zero developed in India (Aryabhata c. 499 CE, Brahmagupta c. 628 CE, *Brahmasphutasiddhanta* formalizing arithmetic with zero). Al-Khwarizmi's *On the Calculation with Hindu Numerals* (c. 820 CE) transmitted the system to the Arab world and **explicitly credits India**. Arabs have always called them *al-arqam al-hindiyya* ("Hindu numerals"). Europeans encountered them via Arab intermediaries (Al-Andalus, Sicily, Fibonacci) and named them after the intermediary rather than the originator. Al-Khwarizmi's own name gave English "algorithm" and "algebra" — so English borrowed from the transmitter in one place (numerals, misattributed) and from the transmitter's *name* in another (algorithm, algebra), in neither case naming the deeper Indian/Babylonian/Greek sources al-Khwarizmi himself drew from.
- **Cyrillic → "Clement-and-Naumic."** Cyril created the **Glagolitic** alphabet (c. 863 CE), not Cyrillic. After Cyril and Methodius died, their disciples were expelled from Great Moravia and welcomed in Bulgaria. There — likely under Clement of Ohrid and Naum, though attribution is contested — they created a new alphabet based on Greek uncial letters, with additional characters (some adapted from Glagolitic) for Slavic-specific sounds. They named this new alphabet after their teacher, even though it was not his design. Glagolitic persisted in Croatian Catholic liturgy into the 20th century; Cyrillic spread everywhere else. Cyril is famous for an alphabet he didn't create; his actual creation is a footnote. **A small honesty in the misattribution**: Cyrillic's Slavic-specific letters (ж, ш, щ, ч, ц) were adapted from Glagolitic, so Cyrillic does inherit Cyril's phonological analysis even though the visible letterforms are Greek. A more accurate English name would be "Clement-and-Naumic," honoring the actual authors. It obviously wouldn't have caught on.
- **Roman alphabet → Etruscan-via-Greek-via-Phoenician alphabet.** Rome inherited the alphabet, much of its early calendar (Etruscan month names), its pantheon (renamed Greek gods — Zeus/Jupiter, Ares/Mars, Aphrodite/Venus), the arch (Etruscan), philosophy and literature (Greek). The Roman synthesis got the fame; the inheritance chain became a footnote even for educated readers.
- **Aztec calendar → Mesoamerican calendar.** The 260-day + 365-day combination is documented at Monte Albán (Zapotec) during the Classical period, centuries before the Mexica arrived in central Mexico as late-coming nomads. The Mexica were late-arriving military consolidators who standardized and elaborated an inherited system. International fame attached to the Mexica version because the Mexica were the dominant power at the moment of Spanish contact — the culture with the high-profile collapse worth writing about.

Dozens of familiar misnomers follow the same structure: *French fries* (Belgian), *English muffin* (American invention), *Jerusalem artichoke* (North American; not from Jerusalem, not an artichoke), *Chinese checkers* (German, originally *Stern-Halma*), *Arabic coffee* (Ethiopian origin, Yemeni cultivation, Arab distribution), *Spanish flu*, *Indian summer*, *Dutch courage*. In each case the name attaches at the namer's point of first encounter, not at the point of true origin.

**Book implication**: this pattern is a recurring motif useful throughout the book rather than a standalone chapter. A chapter on the Roman alphabet can open with its Etruscan-Greek-Phoenician inheritance; a chapter on Cyrillic can correct the Cyril-did-not-make-Cyrillic misattribution early; a chapter on the "Aztec" calendar can restore the Mesoamerican context; the Arabic-numerals case is a good opening anecdote establishing the author's habit of checking who actually invented what. Correcting misnomers is a clean narrative hook in popular nonfiction — readers enjoy the "wait, really?" moment, and the correction builds author credibility for the harder reinterpretations later in the book.

## Tools the Reformer Has Available

Three distinct approaches to how orthography responds to borrowing. Each has its own success conditions:

1. **Lexical replacement.** Coin or revive a native equivalent. *Baladeur* for *Walkman*, *courriel* for *email*, *mot-dièse* for *hashtag*. Success depends on uptake, which depends on prestige. Works when the coined term has independent appeal (succinctness, native sound-feel); fails when it's obviously bureaucratic (*inforoute* for *internet*, rejected by users).
2. **Phonetic re-spelling (nativization).** Adapt the spelling of the loan to match native orthography. *Redingote, boulingrin, bifteck*. Historical in French, dormant for modern loans, proposed for revival by *cràcher*-type reforms.
3. **Accept and mark.** Keep the original spelling as a signal of foreign origin. What French does with most modern English loans now. The *dj* digraph is a compromise between this and phonetic re-spelling: explicit foreign-marker but uses native sound-to-spelling rules.

## Book Project: Advice Summary

### Market Position

Accessible comparative European orthography is underserved. Single-language histories and academic reform studies exist — Horobin on English, Crystal on various; formal linguistic literature on the Greek language question, the Romanian Latinist movement, and so on — but popular cross-European synthesis organized around **what enables or prevents successful reform** hasn't been done well for a general audience. There's room.

### Author Advantages

- **Anthropology training**: multi-frame reading of linguistic artifacts as serving simultaneous political, economic, religious, and prestige functions.
- **French literature**: close-reading skills for decoding policy language, partisan rhetoric, and literary register.
- **Engineering background**: pattern-extraction habit. The frontier/interior/purge typology is exactly the kind of synthesis an academic survey circles around without naming cleanly.
- **Existing blog** at racecondition.software where material can be developed and tested.

### Structure Recommendation

Typology over chronology. Organize by regime:
1. Nationalist archaization (Greek anchor; Faroese, Hebrew, Turkish, Finnish, Catalan satellites)
2. Latinist purification (Romanian anchor; Italian counter-case)
3. Pragmatic reform that succeeded (Italian inheritance, Swedish 1906, Spanish RAE)
4. Failed or partial reform (Danish, French 1990, Greek 1982 monotonic-only)
5. Blank-slate design (Faroese anchor)
6. Closing synthesis: the frontier/interior/purge triage as policy framework for modern reformers.

One anchor case per pattern, satellite cases alongside to show breadth. The closing synthesis pays off the structure by converting the historical survey into a usable framework for thinking about current and future reform.

### Models to Learn From

- **John McWhorter** for voice and accessibility (with Josh's caveat: McWhorter overstates Germanic's divergence from other Indo-European languages, particularly in *Our Magnificent Bastard Tongue* and *What Language Is*; his stronger content claims deserve more skeptical scrutiny than popular reception has given them).
- **Guy Deutscher**, especially *The Unfolding of Language*, for how to make linguistics popular without dumbing down.
- **Mark Forsyth**, *Etymologicon*, for playful scholarship. Forsyth is a journalist rather than a professional linguist and pulls it off — the niche is reachable without an academic perch.

Target voice: McWhorter's accessibility and narrative drive without his occasional empirical overreach. Closer to Deutscher's scholarly caution even when writing for a general audience.

### Practical Steps for the Nine-Year Runway

1. **Use the blog.** Each post tests one chapter's core move. Readers tell you what works and what's opaque. The *cràcher* piece is already a proof of concept for one such move.
2. **Find a sympathetic academic linguist to read drafts.** Not for credentialing — to catch places where the narrative has extrapolated past the evidence. Every popular-linguistics book that lasts has had a reader like this.
3. **Structured language study.** Nine years is enough to gain real reading competence in two or three of the languages covered. Shifts the book from "educated commentary" to "engaged with the material." For a non-specialist author, that shift is what earns critical respect.
4. **Start outlining now.** The hardest thing about a retirement book is arriving at retirement with no habit of writing it. A rough outline revisited annually keeps the pilot light on; the shape will change organically as examples accumulate.

## Josh's Caveat on McWhorter

Josh is a McWhorter fan but believes McWhorter overstates Germanic's divergence from other Indo-European languages. The caveat is worth carrying forward explicitly into the book: **McWhorter's voice and accessibility are models to learn from; his stronger content claims about Germanic's or English's uniqueness deserve more skeptical scrutiny than popular reception has given them.** All languages are "weird" in some respects; singling out a particular family as exceptionally divergent is often a narrative move more than an empirical claim. A book in this space would do well to match McWhorter's craft while avoiding the over-strong claims his more popular work sometimes makes.
